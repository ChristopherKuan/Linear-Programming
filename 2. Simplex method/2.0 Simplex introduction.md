# 2.0 Introduction to Simplex method
<div align='justify'>
  Simplex method, a systematic procedure for solving linear programs. It proceeds by <b>moving from one feasible solution to another, improving the value of the objective function at each step,</b> and finally terminating after a finite number of such transitions.<br><br>

  The simplex method has three characteristics:
  <ol>
    <li>Robust</li>
    It detects redundant constraints, identifies unbounded objective value, and solves problems with one or more optimal solution.<br><br>
    <li>Self-initiating</li>
    It uses itself to either generate an appropriate feasible solution, or to show that the problem has no feasible solution.<br><br>
    <li>Shows more than just optimal solutions</li>
    Its byproducts can indicate how optimal solution varies as a function of the problem data (cost efficients, constraint coefficients, and righthand-side data).
  </ol>

  Before learning how to solve with simplex method, it is important to understand certain prerequisite knowledge.
### Canonical form
Consider the following example linear program:
<table align='center'>
  <tr>
    <td>
      <div align="center">
        Maximize z = 0x<sub>1</sub> + 0x<sub>2</sub> - 3x<sub>3</sub> - 4x<sub>4</sub> +20 (objective 1),
      </div>
      <div align="justify">
        Subject to:
      </div>
      <div align="center">
        x<sub>1</sub> - 3x<sub>3</sub> + 3x<sub>4</sub> = 6 (constraint 1),<br>
        x<sub>2</sub> - 8x<sub>3</sub> + 4x<sub>4</sub> = 4 (constraint 2),<br>
        x<sub>j</sub> (j=1,2,3,4) ≥0 (nonnegative constraint)
      </div>
    </td>
  </tr>
</table>
  This problem is said to be <b>in canonical form has the following characteristics</b>:<br><br>
  <ol>
    <li>All <b>decision variables</b> are constrained to be <b>nonnegative.</b></li><br>
      x<sub>j</sub>≥0 (j=1,2,3,4) (nonnegative constraint)<br><br>
    <li><b>All constraints, except</b> for the <b>nonnegativity</b> of decision variables, are stated as <b>equalities.</b></li><br>
      Constraint 1 and 2 both have '=' sign instead of '≥' and '≤' signs.<br><br>
    <li>The <b>righthand-side</b> coefficients are all <b>nonnegative.</b></li><br>
      Constraint 1 and 2 has righthand-side coefficients of 6 and 4 respectively, both ≥0.<br><br>
    <li><b>One decision variable</b> is <b>isolated in each constraint with a "+1" coefficient.</b> The variable isolated in a given constraint <b>does not appear in any other constraint,</b> and has <b>zero coefficient in the objective function.</b></li><br>
      x<sub>1</sub> in constraint 1 and x<sub>2</sub> in constraint 2 are isolated with "+1" coefficient, they do not appear in other constraints and both have "0" coefficient in objective 1.<br><br>    
  </ol>

## 2.0.1 Optimality Criterion
Using the previous example,
<table align='center'>
  <tr>
    <td>
      <div align="center">
        Maximize z = 0x<sub>1</sub> + 0x<sub>2</sub> - 3x<sub>3</sub> - 4x<sub>4</sub> +20 (objective 1),
      </div>
      <div align="justify">
        Subject to:
      </div>
      <div align="center">
        x<sub>1</sub> - 3x<sub>3</sub> + 3x<sub>4</sub> = 6 (constraint 1),<br>
        x<sub>2</sub> - 8x<sub>3</sub> + 4x<sub>4</sub> = 4 (constraint 2),<br>
        x<sub>j</sub> (j=1,2,3,4) ≥0 (nonnegative constraint)
      </div>
    </td>
  </tr>
</table>
<div align='justify'>
  Observe that, in canonical form, <b>setting x<sub>3</sub> = x<sub>4</sub> = 0</b> immediately gives a <b>feasible solution with x<sub>1</sub> = 6 and x<sub>2</sub> = 4.</b> Solutions like these are known as <b>basic feasible solutions</b>.<br><br>
  There are two types of variables present:
  <ol>
    <li>Basic variable</li>
    Isolated variables that are solved by making them <b>equal to the righthand side constraint.</b> (x<sub>1</sub>, x<sub>2</sub>)<br><br>
    <li>Nonbasic variable</li>
    <b>Variables that are set to zero</b> so basic variables equal to the righthand side constraint. (x<sub>3</sub>, x<sub>4</sub>)<br>
  </ol>
  In general, given a <b>canonical form for any linear program,</b> a <b>basic feasible solution</b> is given by setting the <b>nonbasic variables to zero,</b> and setting the <b>basic variables equal to the righthand side value.</b><br><br>
  Also observe that for any other feasible solution, x<sub>3</sub> and x<sub>4</sub> (nonbasic variables) must remain nonnegative due to the nonnegative constraint. Since their <b>coefficients in the objective function are negative</b>, either x<sub>3</sub> or x<sub>4</sub> being positive will make z<20. Thus, the optimal solution is when x<sub>3</sub> = x<sub>4</sub> = 0. This gives the <b>Optimality Criterion.</b><br><br>
    <table align='center'>
      <tr>
        <td>
          <div align='center'>
            <b>Optimality Criterion</b><br>
          </div>
          <div align='justify'>
            Suppose that, in a <b>maximization problem</b>, <b>every nonbasic variable</b> has a <b>non-positive coefficient</b> in the <b>objective function</b> of a <b>canonical form.</b> Then the <b>basic feasible solution</b> given by the canonical form <b>maximizes the objective function</b> over the feasible region.
          </div>
        </td>
      </tr>
    </table>
</div>

## 2.0.2 Unboundedness Criterion
<div align='justify'>
  Consider the same example, but now in <b>objective function, -3x<sub>3</sub> becomes +3x<sub>3</sub>,</b> so now the objective function becomes "objective 2" as follow:<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        <div align="center">
          Maximize z = 0x<sub>1</sub> + 0x<sub>2</sub> + 3x<sub>3</sub> - 4x<sub>4</sub> +20 (objective 2),
        </div>
        <div align="justify">
          Subject to:
        </div>
        <div align="center">
          x<sub>1</sub> - 3x<sub>3</sub> + 3x<sub>4</sub> = 6 (constraint 1),<br>
          x<sub>2</sub> - 8x<sub>3</sub> + 4x<sub>4</sub> = 4 (constraint 2),<br>
          x<sub>j</sub> (j=1,2,3,4) ≥0 (nonnegative constraint)
        </div>
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table><br>
  Since x<sub>3</sub> has a positive constant in objective 2, increasing value of x<sub>3</sub> will increase the quality of the result.<br><br>
  By maintaining x<sub>4</sub> = 0, increase x<sub>3</sub> to a value of t to be determined, and update x<sub>1</sub> and x<sub>2</sub> to preserve feasibility. Objective 2, constraint 1 and 2 now becomes<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        <div align="center">
          z = 20 + 3t (objective 2),<br>
          x<sub>1</sub> = 6 + 3t (constraint 1),<br>
          x<sub>2</sub> = 4 + 8t (constraint 2),<br>
        </div>
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  From this we can see that <b>as t→∞, z→∞ because both x<sub>1</sub> and x<sub>2</sub> stays positive,</b> so it does not violate the nonnegative constraint. This shows that the <b>objective function is unbounded over the feasible region,</b> giving us the <b>Unboundedness Criterion.</b><br><br>
   <table align='center'>
      <tr>
        <td>
          <div align='center'>
            <b>Unboundedness Criterion</b><br>
          </div>
          <div align='justify'>
            Suppose that, in a <b>maximization problem</b>, <b>some nonbasic variable</b> has a <b>positive coefficient</b> in the <b>objective function</b> of a <b>canonical form.</b> If that nonbasic variable has <b>negative or zero coefficients in all constraints,</b> then the <b>objective function is unbounded</b> from above over the feasible region.
          </div>
        </td>
      </tr>
   </table>
</div>

## 2.0.3 Improvement Criterion
<div align='justify'>
  Now consider another version of the original example, but now in <b>objective function, -4x<sub>4</sub> becomes +x<sub>4</sub>,</b> so now the objective function becomes "objective 3" as follow:<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        <div align="center">
          Maximize z = 0x<sub>1</sub> + 0x<sub>2</sub> - 3x<sub>3</sub> + x<sub>4</sub> +20 (objective 3),
        </div>
        <div align="justify">
          Subject to:
        </div>
        <div align="center">
          x<sub>1</sub> - 3x<sub>3</sub> + 3x<sub>4</sub> = 6 (constraint 1),<br>
          x<sub>2</sub> - 8x<sub>3</sub> + 4x<sub>4</sub> = 4 (constraint 2),<br>
          x<sub>j</sub> (j=1,2,3,4) ≥0 (nonnegative constraint)
        </div>
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>

  Since x<sub>4</sub> has a positive constant in objective 3, increasing value of x<sub>4</sub> will increase value of z.<br><br>
  By maintaining x<sub>3</sub> = 0, increase x<sub>4</sub> to a value of t, and update x<sub>1</sub> and x<sub>2</sub> to preserve feasibility. Objective 2, constraint 1 and 2 now becomes<br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        <div align="center">
          z = 20 + t (objective 3),<br>
          x<sub>1</sub> = 6 - 3t (constraint 1),<br>
          x<sub>2</sub> = 4 - 4t (constraint 2),<br>
        </div>
      </td>

  If x<sub>1</sub> and x<sub>2</sub> were to adhere to nonnegative constraint, it is required that:<br><br>
  <div align='center'>
    6 - 3t ≥ 0, that is t ≤ 2<br>
    4 - 4t ≥ 0, that is t ≤ 1<br><br>
  </div>

  </div>

  This procedure of <b>one basic variable leaving the basis</b> and <b>one nonbasic variable entering the basis</b> is called <b>pivoting</b>, an essential computation of simplex method. In this example, it is said that we have pivoted on x<sub>4</sub> in the second constraint.<br>

  Consequently, after pivoting, the form of the problem has been altered, but the <b>modified equations still represent the original problem.</b> They still have the <b>same feasible solutions and same objective value</b> when evaluated at any given feasible solution.<br>

  In summary, the basic step for generating a canonical form with an improved value for objective function is described as the <b>Improvement Criterion.</b><br>

  <table align='center'>

          </div>

            Suppose that, in a <b>maximization problem</b>, <b>some nonbasic variable</b> has a <b>positive coefficient</b> in the <b>objective function</b> of a <b>canonical form.</b> If that nonbasic variable has a <b>positive coefficient in some constraint,</b> then a new <b>basic feasible solution</b> may be obtained by <b>pivoting</b>.
          </div>
        </td>
      </tr>
   </table>
</div>
  
## 2.0.4 Ratio and Pivoting Criterion
<div align='justify'>
  During pivoting, a basic variable has to be chose to leave the basis. The basic variable that first goes to 0 when the nonbasic variable increases is chosen, as it is the limiting factor. Taking the previous example in 2.0.4, the current basic variables have to be ≥0:<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        for $x_1 \Rightarrow 6 - 3x_4 \ge 0 \Rightarrow x_4 \le \frac{6}{3} \Rightarrow x_4 \le 2$
      </td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>
        for $x_2 \Rightarrow 4 - 4x_4 \ge 0 \Rightarrow x_4 \le \frac{4}{4} \Rightarrow x_4 \le 1$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  We can see that the limiting factor is x<sub>4</sub> ≤ 1 is the limiting factor, making x<sub>2</sub> the basic variable to exit the basis. Formally, the constraint can be selected by taking the <b>ratio of the righthand-side coefficients to the coefficients of the nonbasic variable entering the constraints</b>, known as the <b>ratio test:</b>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        min { $\frac{RHS\,coeff\,1}{nonbasic\,coeff\,constraint\,1}, ..., \frac{RHS\,coeff\,n}{nonbasic\,coeff\,constraint\,n}$ }
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>

  Note that, if the coefficient of x<sub>4</sub> in the second constraint <b>becomes negative:</b>
  <div align = 'center'>
    x<sub>2</sub> - 8x<sub>3</sub> + 4x<sub>4</sub> = 4 → x<sub>2</sub> - 8x<sub>3</sub> - 4x<sub>4</sub> = 4
  </div><br>
  Then now the basic variables values will be given by:<br><br>
  <div align = 'center'>
    x<sub>1</sub> = 6 - 3x<sub>4</sub><br>
    x<sub>2</sub> = 4 + 4x<sub>4</sub>
  </div><br>
  We can see that x<sub>4</sub> → ∞ will <b>not violate the nonnegativity constraint</b> of the basic variable x<sub>2</sub>. This shows that <b>only compute the ratio test</b> for the constraint where the <b>nonbasic variable has positive coefficient.</b> This gives the <b>Ratio and Pivoting Criterion:</b><br><br>

  <table align='center'>
      <tr>
        <td>
          <div align='center'>
            <b>Ratio and Pivoting Criterion</b><br>
          </div>
          <div align='justify'>
            When improving a given canonical form by introducing nonbasic variable x<sub>s</sub> into the basis, <b>pivot in a constraint</b> that gives the <b>minimum ratio of righthand-side coefficient to corresponsing x<sub>s</sub> coefficient.</b> Compute these ration only for constraints that have a <b>positive coefficient for x<sub>s</sub>.</b>
          </div>
        </td>
      </tr>
   </table>
</div>

## 2.0.5 Conclusion
<div align='justify'> The simplex method provides a systematic and efficient way to solve linear programming problems by moving from one basic feasible solution to another while improving the objective value. By expressing problems in canonical form and applying the optimality, unboundedness, improvement, and ratio criteria, the method ensures feasibility is maintained and guides the selection of entering and leaving variables. Overall, it offers both a clear computational procedure and valuable insights into the structure and behavior of optimization problems. In the next section, we will see the simplex method in action.</div>
