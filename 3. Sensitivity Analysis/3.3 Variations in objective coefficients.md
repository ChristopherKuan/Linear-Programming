# 3.3 Variations in Objective Coefficients
<div align='justify'>
  Previously, we have looked at shadown prices and reduced cost, which are due to <b>changes in the RHS of constraints and nonnegativity constraints.</b> Now let us look at how much can <b>objective function coefficients</b> vary before changing the values of <b>decision variables</b> in the optimal solution.<br><br>
  Changes should be made <b>one at a time,</b> holding all <b>other coefficients and RHS values constant.</b><br><br>
  Let us consider the same example as before from <b>3.1 Shadow Prices</b> and <b>3.2 Reduced cost</b>:<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        Maximize z = 5x<sub>1</sub> + 4.5x<sub>2</sub> + 6x<sub>3</sub> (hundreds of dollars)
        <div align='left'>
          subject to:
        </div>
        6x<sub>1</sub> + 5x<sub>2</sub> + 8x<sub>3</sub> ≤ 60 (production capacity; hours)<br>
        10x<sub>1</sub> + 20x<sub>2</sub> + 10x<sub>3</sub> ≤ 150 (warehouse capacity; hundreds if sq. ft.)<br>
        x<sub>1</sub> ≤ 8 (demand for glass x<sub>1</sub>)
        x<sub>1</sub> ≥ 0, x<sub>2</sub> ≥ 0, x<sub>3</sub> ≥ 0<br>
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  We will look at two cases, changing the coefficient of a <b>nonbasic and basic variable.</b>
</div>

## 3.3.3 General formulation
### Nonbasic variable
<div align='justify'>
  Recall that the <b>initial objective function coefficient</b> of x<sub>j</sub> is denoted by:<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        $$c_j^{new} = c_j + \Delta c_j$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  If x<sub>j</sub> is a nonbasic variable, and its objective-function coefficient c<sub>j</sub> can be changed by a small amount, with all other data held fixed. The <b>current solution remains unchanged</b> as long as the <b>new reduced cost (recall it is the final objective function coefficient) remains nonpositive,</b> shown below:<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>
        $$\bar{c}_j^{new} = c_j^{new} - \sum_{i=1}^m a_{ij}y_i = c_j+ \Delta c_j - \sum_{i=1}^m a_{ij}y_i = \bar{c}_j + \Delta c_j \leq 0$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  Note that the reduced cost general equation used in the previous chapter <b>3.2 Reduced cost</b> has been used to assist the transition into the final formulation.<br><br>
  From the equation, we can see that the <b>range of variation for the small change</b> of the original objective function coefficient of x<sub>j</sub> is capped by the <b>negative of its original reduced cost:</b><br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        $$- \infty \lt \Delta c_j \leq - \bar{c}_j$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  And the <b>range of the initial objective function coefficient (c<sub>j</sub><sup>new</sup>) is:</b><br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>
        $$- \infty \lt c_j^{new} \leq c_j - \bar{c}_j$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  If that <b>range is exceeded,</b> then this nonbasic variable x<sub>j</sub> will <b>enter the basis.</b>
</div>

### Basic variable
<div align='justify'>
  Now consider basic variable <b>x<sub>r</sub> in row k</b>. Let the <b>original objective-function coefficient c<sub>r</sub></b> be given by<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        $$c_r^{new} = c_r + \Delta c_r$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  change by a small amount, while all other data are fixed. The <b>reduced cost of x<sub>r</sub></b> is given by:<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>
        $$\bar{c}_r^{new} = c_r^{new} - \sum_{i=1}^m a_{ir}y_i = c_r + \Delta c_r - \sum_{i=1}^m a_{ir}y_i = \bar{c}_r + \Delta c_r$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  Since x<sub>r</sub> is a basic variable,
  <table align='center'>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>
        $$\bar{c}_r = 0$$
      </td>
    </tr>
    <tr><td></td></tr>
    <tr align='left'>
      <td>
        and we need to make $$\bar{c}_r^{new} = 0$$ by subtracting $$\Delta c_r$$ x $$k_{th}$$ constraint in the <b>final tableau</b> from the <b>final objective function.</b>
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  This gives the <b>new reduced cost for all nonbasic variables as</b><br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>
        $$\bar{c}_j^{new} = \bar{c}_j - \Delta c_r \bar{a}_{kj}$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  Or in other words, the new reduced cost for nonbasic variable x<sub>j</sub> is the <b>original reduced cost for x<sub>j</sub> - (change of c<sub>r</sub> * original coefficient of x<sub>j</sub> in row k.</b><br><br>
  The current <b>basis remains</b> if the <b>new reduced cost for all nonbasic variable ≤ 0.</b> Thus the <b>range of variation of the objective function coefficient</b> is:<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>
        $$Max_j \left\{ \left. \frac{\bar{c}_j}{\bar{a}_{kj}} \right| \bar{a}_{kj} \gt 0 \right\} \leq \Delta c_r \leq Min_j \left\{ \left. \frac{\bar{c}_j}{\bar{a}_{kj}} \right| \bar{a}_{kj} \lt 0 \right\}$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  Or in layman terms, the <b>small change applied</b> to the original objective function coefficient of basic variable x<sub>r</sub> in k<sub>th</sub> constraint has to be greater than or equal to the <b>maximum among all ratio of original reduced cost of nonbasic variable x<sub>j</sub> to the original coefficient of x<sub>j</sub> in constraint k in the final tableau, given that the coefficient of x<sub>j</sub> is positive,</b> and it has to be lesser than or equal to the <b>minimum among all ratio of original reduced cost of nonbasic variable x<sub>j</sub> to the original coefficient of x<sub>j</sub> in constraint k in the final tableau, given that the coefficient of x<sub>j</sub> is negative.</b><br><br>
  The variable <b>entering the basis</b> is the variable <b>giving the limiting values</b> for the range of variation of the objective function coefficient. The variable <b>leaving the basis</b> is then determined by the <b>ratio test.</b>
</div>

## 3.3.4 Conclusion
<div align='justify'>
  This section shows that the <b>optimal solution of a linear programming problem is not immediately sensitive to small changes in objective function coefficients.</b> Instead, there exists a <b>range of optimality</b> within which the current basis — and therefore the values of the decision variables — remains unchanged.<br><br>

  For a <b>nonbasic variable</b>, the condition is straightforward: its <b>reduced cost must remain nonpositive.</b> This ensures that the variable does not become attractive enough to enter the basis. Hence, the allowable change is directly tied to its <b>original reduced cost.</b><br><br>

  For a <b>basic variable</b>, the analysis is more involved because any change affects the reduced costs of <b>all nonbasic variables.</b> The allowable range is therefore determined by ensuring that <b>all updated reduced costs remain nonpositive,</b> leading to a set of bounds defined by ratios involving tableau coefficients.<br><br>

  Overall, this analysis highlights an important insight: <b>optimality depends not only on the objective coefficients themselves, but on their relationship with the structure of the final tableau.</b> Understanding these ranges allows decision-makers to assess how robust their solution is and anticipate when a change in coefficients will lead to a <b>different optimal basis</b> and thus a different decision strategy.
</div>
