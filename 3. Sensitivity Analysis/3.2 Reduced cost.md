# 3.2 Reduced cost
<div align='justify'>
  Previously, we looked at shadow prices, and they are associated with constraints. What about shadow prices associated with nonnegativity constraint? These are known as <i>reduced costs.</i><br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr align='justify'>
      <td>
        <div align='center'>
          Reduced cost
        </div><br>
        Reduced cost associated with the <b>nonnegativity constraint</b> for each variable is the <b>shadow price of that constraint.</b> It means the corresponding change in the objective function per unit increase in the lower bound of the nonnegativity of the variable.
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
</div>

## 3.2.1 Determining reduced cost
<div align='justify'>
  There are two ways to determine the reduced cost of a nonnegative variable.<br><br>
  Consider the same example from <b>3.1 Shadow Prices:</b><br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        Maximize z = 5x<sub>1</sub> + 4.5x<sub>2</sub> + 6x<sub>3</sub> (hundreds of dollars)
        <div align='left'>
          subject to:
        </div>
        6x<sub>1</sub> + 5x<sub>2</sub> + 8x<sub>3</sub> ≤ 60 (production capacity; hours)<br>
        10x<sub>1</sub> + 20x<sub>2</sub> + 10x<sub>3</sub> ≤ 150 (warehouse capacity; hundreds if sq. ft.)<br>
        x<sub>1</sub> ≤ 8 (demand for glass x<sub>1</sub>)
        x<sub>1</sub> ≥ 0, x<sub>2</sub> ≥ 0, x<sub>3</sub> ≥ 0<br>
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
</div>

### 3.2.1.2 Pricing out an activity
<div align='justify'>
  The operation of determining the reduced cost of an activity from the <b>shadow price and the objective function</b> is genrally referred to as <b>pricing out an activity.</b><br><br>
  A new concept is introduced here - <b>opportunity cost.</b><br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr align='justify'>
      <td>
        <div align='center'>
          Opportunity cost
        </div><br>
        The value of resources given up by using them for one activity instead of the best alternative.
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  Recall that the final tableau are as follow:<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr align='center'>
      <th>Basic variables</th>
      <th>Current values</th>
      <th>x<sub>1</sub></th>
      <th>x<sub>2</sub></th>
      <th>x<sub>3</sub></th>
      <th>x<sub>4</sub></th>
      <th>x<sub>5</sub></th>
      <th>x<sub>6</sub></th>
    </tr>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>x<sub>2</sub></td>
      <td>$$4 \frac{2}{7}$$</td>
      <td></td>
      <td>1</td>
      <td>$$- \frac{2}{7}$$</td>
      <td>$$- \frac{1}{7}$$</td>
      <td>$$\frac{3}{35}$$</td>
      <td></td>
    </tr>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>x<sub>6</sub></td>
      <td>$$1 \frac{4}{7}$$</td>
      <td></td>
      <td></td>
      <td>$$- \frac{11}{7}$$</td>
      <td>$$- \frac{2}{7}$$</td>
      <td>$$\frac{1}{14}$$</td>
      <td>1</td>
    </tr>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>x<sub>1</sub></td>
      <td>$$6 \frac{3}{7}$$</td>
      <td>1</td>
      <td></td>
      <td>$$\frac{11}{7}$$</td>
      <td>$$\frac{2}{7}$$</td>
      <td>$$- \frac{1}{14}$$</td>
      <td></td>
    </tr>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>(-z)</td>
      <td>$$-51 \frac{3}{7}$$</td>
      <td></td>
      <td></td>
      <td>$$- \frac{4}{7}$$</td>
      <td>$$- \frac{11}{14}$$</td>
      <td>$$- \frac{1}{35}$$</td>
      <td></td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  Let us consider x<sub>3</sub>. From the intial constraints we can see that producing x<sub>3</sub> requires <b>8 production hours</b>, and <b>10 hundred cubic storage capacity.</b> The opportunity cost of these two are <b> 11/14 and 1/35</b> respectively. This brings the <b>total opportunity cost of producing x<sub>3</sub> is:</b><br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        $$( \frac{11}{14} )8 + ( \frac{1}{35} )10 = 6 \frac{4}{7}$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  We can see that the opportunity cost of production hours and storage capacity are the negative coefficients of x<sub>4</sub> and x<sub>5</sub> in the final tableau, multiplied by the initial coefficient of the variable that is considered. In other words, given that there are <b>m constraints</b>:<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>
        Opportunity cost of x<sub>n</sub> = $$\sum_{i=1}^{m}$$(Shadow Price of constraint i * initial coefficient of x<sub>n</sub> in constraint i)
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  Now, the <b>contribution</b> of x<sub>3</sub> would be the <b>initial objective function coefficient</b> of x<sub>3</sub>, which is 6 in this case.<br><br>
  The reduced cost can be calculated with the following formula:<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>
        Reduced cost = Contribution - Opportunity cost
      </td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td></td>
    </tr>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>
        This brings the reduced cost of x<sub>3</sub> = $$6 - 6 \frac{4}{7} = - \frac{4}{7}$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
</div>

## 3.2.2 Interpreting reduced cost
<div align='justify'>
   Reduced cost of a variable is exactly its <b>coefficient in the objective row of the final tableau</b>, this means that when it is:<br><br>
  <ul>
    <li>Negative</li>
    This means that the item is not profitable, so it <b>stays at 0, not entering the basis.</b><br><br>
    <li>Positive</li>
    This means that the item improves the objective, so it <b>enters the basis.</b><br><br>
  </ul>
</div>

  <ul>
    <li>x<sub>1</sub>, x<sub>2</sub>, ..., x<sub>n</sub></li>
    Decision variables.<br><br>
    <li>x<sub>n+1</sub>, x<sub>n+2</sub>, ..., x<sub>n+m</sub></li>
    Slack / artificial variables introduced to transform problem into canonical form.<br><br>
    <li>a<sub>mn</sub></li>
    Coefficient of decision variable n in constraint m.<br><br>
    <li>b<sub>m</sub></li>
    RHS of constraint m.<br><br>
    <li>c<sub>n</sub> </li>
    Objective function coefficient of decision variables, and slack variables have zero contribution.
    <li>y<sub>m</sub></li>
    Shadow prices of constraint m.
  </ul>
  Now assume that the <b>optimal solution</b> to this problem has been found and the corresponding <b>final form of the objective function</b> is:<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        $$(-z) + \bar{c}_1 x_1 + \bar{c}_2 x_2 + ... + \bar{c}_n x_n + \bar{c}_{n+1} x_{n+1} + \bar{c}_{n+2} x_{n+2} + ... + \bar{c}_{n+m} x_{n+m} = - \bar{z}_0$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  where the <b>c with a bar on top</b> means the <b>updated coefficient</b> of the variables.<br><br>
  Recall in <b>3.1 Shadow Prices</b> that shadow prices are <b>negative of the coefficient</b> of the appropriate <b>slack (or artificial) variable</b> in the <b>objective function of the final tableau.</b> This means that:
  <table align='center'>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>
        $$\bar{c}_{n+1} = -y_1$$, $$\bar{c}_{n+2} = -y_2$$, ..., $$\bar{c}_{n+m} = -y_m$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  Also, we know that <b>reduced cost of a variable is the contribution - opportunity cost</b> (sum of all shadow price of constraint multiplied by the initial coefficient of that variable in the constraint), this gives the <b>reduced cost of variable x<sub>j</sub> as:</b><br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr>
      <td>
        $$\bar{c}_j = c_j - \sum_{i=1}^{m} a_{ij}y_i$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  Finally, this gives the <b>current value of objective function</b> as:<br><br>
  <table align='center'>
    <tr align='center'>
      <td>
        $$- \bar{z}_0 = -\sum_{i=1}^{m} \bar{c}_i = \sum_{i=1}^{m}$$
      </td>
    </tr>
  </table>
  because each constraint represents a resource, and y<sub>i</sub> is the value per unit of that resource and b<sub>i</sub> tells you how much of that resource you have.
</div>

## 3.2.4 Conclusion
<div align='justify'>
  Reduced cost provides a powerful way to evaluate whether a variable should enter the solution by comparing its direct contribution to the objective function with the opportunity cost of the resources it consumes. It can be determined either directly from the final tableau or through shadow prices, showing the deep connection between primal and dual interpretations. A variable with negative reduced cost (in a maximization problem) is not worth introducing because its resource usage outweighs its benefit, while a positive reduced cost indicates potential improvement. Overall, reduced cost links the ideas of shadow prices, opportunity cost, and optimality, giving both a computational tool and an economic interpretation of the simplex solution.
</div>
